Skate, stingray or eagle ray — how to tell them apart

Skates, stingrays and eagle rays are all flat-bodied cartilaginous fish (batoids) and easy to mix up. Focus on three things — body shape, tail and dorsal fins — and they come apart quickly.

🟤 Skate

  • Diamond- or heart-shaped disc.
  • Tail short, thick and fleshy, with no venomous spine; usually two small dorsal fins near the tip.
  • Blunt thorns along the back for defence.
  • Egg-laying — the rectangular “mermaid's purse” cases you find on beaches.

🔵 Stingray

  • Rounded disc with smooth margins.
  • Tail long and whip-like, armed with a serrated, venomous spine; dorsal fin reduced or absent.
  • Gives birth to live young instead of laying eggs.
  • Often lies half-buried in the sand.

⚫ Eagle ray

  • Distinct, protruding duck-bill head set off from the body.
  • Pointed, wing-like fins — it “flies” through open water instead of hugging the bottom.
  • Most carry a venomous tail spine, like their stingray cousins.
  • Not the same as a manta (family Mobulidae): the manta is huge, filter-feeds and has lost its sting — a close cousin, not an eagle ray.

One-line memory aid

Skate = fleshy tail, no sting, lays eggs; stingray = whip tail with a venomous barb; eagle ray = a “flying” wing-beater with a beaked head.
